Methodology

A consistent basket, measured against national or state earning capacity

The index is designed solely for comparability, not exhaustive household budgeting. It uses products and services that can be matched closely across markets and keeps every modelling decision visible in the source ledger available on request.

City coverage

A consistent population-based sample

For each covered European country, KISoL includes the two largest cities by population. The United States is represented by its ten largest cities by population.

The main website sample excludes European microstates to reduce structural outliers from unusually small labor, housing, and cross-border markets. Luxembourg remains included, while the full-sample workbook retains the excluded microstates for audit.

Framework

Three related views

Basket cost

Shows where the standardized collection of prices is cheaper or more expensive. Income and employment do not enter this view.

City affordability

Compares a European national or U.S. state-adjusted net-median earning input, adjusted for the corresponding prime-age employment share, with the same city price basket.

Country and state affordability

Population-weights the sampled city baskets within each European country or U.S. state, then compares the grouped basket with the applicable national or state labor input.

Country and state aggregation

Population weights across the sampled cities

City population weight

Population of the city divided by the total population of all sampled cities in the same country or U.S. state.

Geography basket

For each price component, sum the city component multiplied by its population weight. Apply the same fixed fuel, housing, and goods weights to those grouped components.

Geography affordability index

Divide applicable income capacity by the population-weighted basket, then rebase that ratio to the median across all countries and states in the grouped sample.

Weights sum to 100% within the sampled cities only. A U.S. state represented by one city receives a 100% city weight. The result does not estimate prices for places outside the sampled cities.

Price basket

Comparable, sensible, standardized items

The basket uses fixed categories and fixed internal weights. Product identities are generally described by specification rather than brand.

Category What is compared Why it is included
Road fuel The same commonly purchased grade, converted to a common volume and currency. A standardized, frequently updated proxy for energy- and transport-related cost pressure across local services. It avoids comparing quality-sensitive service quotes while retaining a channel that affects delivery and operating costs.
Housing Newly advertised rents and purchase prices per unit of floor area. Measures the price a new entrant faces rather than protected or long-standing incumbent contracts.
Basic food A consistent mix of animal protein, staple carbohydrate, vegetable, dairy, and eggs. Covers ordinary nutrition while avoiding cuisine-specific household baskets.
Standardized car A Korean-made compact car sold widely with closely comparable quality, observed both new and used. Tests the cost of a globally tradable durable good and local vehicle-market frictions.
Consumer technology The same current premium smartphone with the same storage tier. Provides a highly standardized globally traded product with transparent official pricing.
Eating out A standardized chicken portion from the same international quick-service chain. Acts as a repeatable proxy for prepared food, labor, commercial rent, and local operating costs.
Footwear The same classic white leather sneaker from the same international manufacturer. Adds a standardized apparel product with identical design and quality across markets.
Why fuel is used as a quality-consistent service-price proxy Open evidence and audit

Fuel is standardized, local, frequently priced, and difficult to disguise with differences in presentation or service quality. Its retail price combines a common energy input with local taxes, distribution costs, and margins. Energy and transport also enter service production directly and through supply chains. The ECB's input-output evidence documents direct and indirect energy costs in service-sector output; Falvey and Gemmell find that higher tradable prices are associated with higher service prices; and Gelb and Diofasi's 168-economy analysis finds that subsidy programmes, measured partly through fuel subsidies, help explain price-level differences beyond income.

Quality is costly. Better staff, more time, better premises, better materials, and a credible reputation require resources. Rosen links observed prices to product characteristics. Klein and Leffler show that price premiums can finance and enforce performance when quality is difficult to observe. Shapiro derives an equilibrium price-quality schedule in which high quality commands a premium that pays for reputation. Higher service prices therefore tend to be directionally associated with higher quality, although not mechanically. A fixed fuel grade avoids much of the quality noise in unmatched service quotes.

Direct validation with three services

The test uses the 28 European countries shared by all three service datasets. The first measure is the same fixed prepared-food portion from one international quick-service chain. The second is the counter price of an ordinary domestic standard letter using the slower D+X product. The third is the average lowest standard men's haircut price from highly rated central-city salons. Wages, employment, housing, and every other basket component are excluded.

Prepared-food service: Pc = arithmetic mean of sampled city prices in country c, in USD.

Postal service: Lc = domestic standard-letter counter price in country c, in EUR.

Haircut service: Hc = arithmetic mean of published source-city haircut prices in country c, in EUR.

P*c = Pc / GM(P),   L*c = Lc / GM(L),   H*c = Hc / GM(H)

Combined service proxy: Sc = 100 × ∛(P*c × L*c × H*c)

GM is the geometric mean across the 28-country sample. Each service price is divided by its own sample geometric mean, removing differences in currencies and absolute price scales. The three normalized price relatives are then combined geometrically, giving each service equal proportional weight and preventing any one price level from dominating the proxy. Countries are divided at the sample median fuel price of $7.38 per gallon.

KISoL uses fuel because it is a strong, quality-consistent proxy for local service prices. The relationship is directional rather than one-for-one, so the fuel weight remains an explicit index design choice rather than an estimated causal coefficient.

Complete calculation

The index formulas

Each price is converted to a common currency and normalized against the sample median for the same item before the components are combined. Exact product identities and numerical weights remain in the audit workbook; the public formula shows the complete calculation structure.

Notation: c means the city being evaluated, and x means one specific basket item.

Step 1 - raw income-adjusted city affordability

Raw City Affordabilityc = Applicable Net Median Monthly Wagec × Applicable Prime-Age Employment Coefficientc Weighted City Price Basketc

This is the affordability ratio before rebasing. European cities use the applicable national wage and employment coefficient; U.S. cities use the applicable state-adjusted wage and state employment coefficient.

Step 2 - rebased income-adjusted city affordability

City Affordability Indexc = 100 × Raw City Affordabilityc Median Raw City Affordability across all sampled cities

Higher values mean greater earning capacity relative to the standardized city basket. The sample median equals 100.

Basket-only city cost

City Basket Cost Indexc = 100 × Weighted City Price Basketc Median Weighted City Price Basket across all sampled cities

Lower values mean a cheaper basket. Income and employment do not enter this index.

Expanded weighted city basket

Weighted City Price Basketc =
  • Fuel Weight × Road Fuel Relative Pricec
  • Housing Weight × [(Asking Rent Relative Pricec + Asking Purchase Price Relativec) ÷ 2]
  • Goods Weight × [(Food Relative Costc + Used Standardized Car Relative Pricec + New Standardized Car Relative Pricec + Premium Smartphone Relative Pricec + Prepared-Food Relative Pricec + Standardized Footwear Relative Pricec) ÷ Number of Goods Components]

The three top-level weights sum to one. Food is itself an equal-weight average of the standardized animal-protein, staple-carbohydrate, vegetable, dairy, and egg price relatives.

Normalization and supporting variables

Relative Price of Item x in City c
Price of Item x in City c ÷ Median Price of Item x across all sampled cities.
Price in Common Currency
Price in Local Currency ÷ Local-Currency Units per Common-Currency Unit.
Road Fuel Price per Gallon
(Road Fuel Price in Local Currency per Litre ÷ Local-Currency Units per Common-Currency Unit) × Litres per Gallon.
Food Relative Costc
Arithmetic mean of the five standardized food-item relative prices in City c.
Applicable Net Median Monthly Wagec
The national net-median wage assigned to a European city, or the state-adjusted net-median wage assigned to a U.S. city.
Applicable Prime-Age Employment Coefficientc
The corresponding national or U.S. state prime-age employment rate expressed as a decimal.
Raw City Affordabilityc
(Applicable Net Median Monthly Wagec × Applicable Prime-Age Employment Coefficientc) ÷ Weighted City Price Basketc.

Calculation

How the comparison is built

Convert

Every local-currency observation is converted to a common currency using the same market exchange-rate snapshot.

Normalize

Each individual price is divided by the median for that same item across the city sample.

Combine

Like-for-like prices are combined into food, standardized goods, housing, and fuel components using fixed weights.

Rebase

The resulting basket and affordability measures are rebased so the sample median is the common reference value of 100.

Income capacity

Why the affordability numerator uses broader labor markets

European cities use the national net median monthly wage and national prime-age employment share. U.S. cities use a state-adjusted wage input and the corresponding state prime-age employment share. The same applicable labor capacity is assigned to sampled cities in the same country or U.S. state.

Comparable city wage medians and city prime-age employment rates are rarely available. Published city averages often mix different industries, omit commuters, disagree on city boundaries, and create a level of precision the underlying data cannot support.

Sources

Direct sources first

Inputs are anchored to official statistics, fuel-price publishers, local real-estate portals, supermarket or retailer pages, official product stores, and relevant vehicle marketplaces. Current direct observations are preferred; transformations, fallbacks, and modelled values are labelled in the source ledger.

No crowd-sourced cost-of-living aggregator, such as Numbeo.com, is used as a data source.

Exclusions

What is deliberately left out

Healthcare and dentistry are excluded because subsidies, insurance structures, access rules, waiting times, and quality differences make sticker prices misleading across countries.

The index also does not attempt to represent visa rules, crime, education, childcare, climate, language, household size, personal savings, or individual consumption preferences.
