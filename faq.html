Frequently asked questions

Why this index is built differently

The design favors comparable market-entry prices and transparent assumptions over broad macroeconomic averages or apparently precise city estimates.

Why not use purchasing power parity?

KISoL rejects purchasing power parity as a general measure of purchasing power or living standards. Purchasing power exists in relation to particular things: housing, food, imported technology, transport, savings, travel, or a specific household's consumption. PPP compresses many unlike prices into one conversion factor and then encourages that conditional result to be read as if it were purchasing power in the abstract.

The aggregate depends on choices rather than a uniquely observable price level. The World Bank's International Comparison Program methodology requires a selected basket, judgments about whether products are both comparable and representative, national expenditure weights, and methods for linking regional calculations into a global result. Change the basket, weights, reference structure, quality treatment, or aggregation rule and measured real income can change even though nobody's actual income or consumption has changed.

The work of Bela Balassa in the Journal of Political Economy and Paul Samuelson in The Review of Economics and Statistics explains another problem. Productivity differences in internationally traded sectors can produce systematically cheaper local services in lower-income economies. A haircut, domestic cleaning hour, or locally provided service may therefore make a country look substantially richer after PPP conversion, but that price advantage cannot be used to buy an imported phone, vehicle, machinery, foreign education, or assets priced in world markets.

This is the basis of the deliberately sharp phrase “poverty polished properly.” Cheap local labor can improve a broad PPP-adjusted income statistic while access to globally traded goods, capital, secure housing, or high-quality services remains constrained. The phrase is polemical, but the underlying tradable versus non-tradable distinction is a standard result of international economics.

The practical evidence is also cautionary. Angus Deaton and Alan Heston document problems involving quality differences, urban and rural prices, government services, health, education, and the regional construction of global PPPs. Deaton's 2010 American Economic Review article concludes that PPP comparisons between very different countries have weak theoretical and empirical foundations. His earlier World Bank Research Observer study shows that revisions to PPP exchange rates can sharply change poverty estimates for reasons unrelated to the lived experience of poor households. Sanjay Reddy and Thomas Pogge similarly argue that a general purchasing-power equivalence is not well defined for poverty assessment unless it is anchored to the goods required to meet concrete human needs.

KISoL therefore does not attempt to estimate a comprehensive national purchasing power. It asks a narrower question, fixes the compared categories, observes current market-entry prices, keeps globally traded goods visible, and publishes the weighting rule. This sacrifices the apparent completeness of PPP in exchange for a result whose meaning can be stated and audited.

Can one number represent an overall price level?

An overall price level is not observed in the way that the price of fuel or a square metre of rent is observed. (Even a square metre of rent is not perfectly uniform: building age, construction quality, insulation, heating and air conditioning, lift access, furnishing, maintenance, power reliability, neighbourhood amenities, and transport access all change what that square metre provides.) It is an index constructed from many prices that move by different amounts and sometimes in opposite directions. Producing one number requires decisions about which goods count, whose purchases supply the weights, how quality changes are treated, which period or country is the reference, and which mathematical formula combines the observations.

In The Theory of Money and Credit, Ludwig von Mises argued that index numbers can assist historical and statistical description but should not be treated as an exact, purpose-independent measure of money's purchasing power. Different people purchase different combinations of goods, so a price index necessarily answers a question defined by its selected basket and weights.

Friedrich Hayek's 1974 Nobel lecture adds a related aggregation warning: economy-wide totals can conceal the dispersed circumstances and changing relative prices that coordinate individual decisions. Two economies may display the same aggregate price index while having very different structures of housing costs, wages, shortages, service quality, and tradable-goods prices.

Modern index-number research reaches a more technical version of the same caution. Erwin Diewert's comment on PPP-based national accounts treats the issue explicitly as one of index numbers and aggregation. Different formulas satisfy different desirable properties, but no formula removes the need to choose a basket, weights, and comparison purpose.

This does not mean that aggregation is never informative. It means that the resulting number should be described as a model output rather than “the” uniquely true price level. KISoL is also an aggregate, so it does not claim immunity from this criticism. Its response is to remain deliberately narrow, hold the categories constant, disclose the formula, and publish both the component prices and the final index.

Why not compare nominal wages?

A nominal wage says nothing about what that income can buy. A higher salary can be fully offset by more expensive housing, fuel, food, or standardized goods. Nominal wage rankings therefore tend to place high-income economies at the top even when the local cost burden is also high.

Why not rank cities only by prices?

Price-only results answer a different question: where is the fixed basket cheapest? They do not measure whether local earning capacity can comfortably pay for it. The website therefore provides both the basket-cost ranking and the income-adjusted affordability ranking.

The two views serve different readers. The affordability ranking is the closer lens for a non-incumbent person who plans to move and earn locally. The basket-only ranking is useful for someone bringing income from elsewhere, including a remote worker.

Why use national or state-level wages instead of city wages?

Comparable city-level net-median wage inputs are not available for most of the sample. Available figures often use averages rather than medians, cover different years, omit informal work, and reflect different industry mixes.

National evidence is not uniform either. KISoL therefore labels direct net medians separately from gross-to-net conversions, subgroup proxies, and other documented estimates in the workbook source ledger.

City boundaries also fail to match labor markets. A person may live in a suburb and work in the central city, while the wage survey and housing observation classify that person differently. Broader national or state data avoids presenting these incompatible estimates as if they were precise.

Comparable prime-age employment rates are also generally unavailable at city level. Even where they exist, commuters can live outside the administrative city while contributing to its labor market, incomes, and prices. Combining a city wage estimate with a differently bounded city employment rate would compound the mismatch.

What about people who commute or shop across borders?

Commuting and shopping can connect the income of one place with the prices of another. Someone may live in New Jersey and work in New York, or live in Bratislava and work in Vienna. Residents of a higher-price city may also shop in a nearby lower-price city, influencing demand and prices on both sides of the border.

Using national labor inputs reduces this boundary problem when the commute occurs within the same country, because the worker remains inside the same wage and employment geography. It cannot solve cross-country cases such as Vienna and Bratislava. U.S. state-level inputs also leave an interstate limitation in cases such as New Jersey and New York.

KISoL cannot adjust these flows consistently because comparable origin-and-destination data for commuting, earnings, and retail spending are not available for every city pair in the sample. Results for closely connected border cities should therefore be read with additional caution.

Does using national income erase real city differences?

It deliberately assigns the same income capacity to European cities in the same country and to U.S. cities in the same state. Their ranking difference then comes from city prices, especially housing. This is a limitation, but it is visible and consistent. An unsupported city-wage estimate would introduce hidden error while appearing more sophisticated.

Because KISoL is primarily a European project, the national approach is somewhat easier to defend for much of the sample: most European countries are smaller than the United States and their wage, tax, and statistical systems are organized nationally. It remains a simplification, particularly in larger and more regionally diverse countries.

The United States was the clearest problem, so this edition uses state-level inputs. Each U.S. city receives a state-adjusted net-median wage based on the BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages state-to-U.S. wage ratio and a direct BLS state prime-age employment coefficient. Cities in the same state share those inputs.

The state wage remains an estimate: QCEW reports average gross payroll and is used only as a geographic ratio to scale the existing national net-median anchor. It is not presented as a directly published state net median.

Why do secondary cities usually outrank the main city in the same country?

For European cities, KISoL assigns the same national net-median wage and prime-age employment coefficient to both sampled cities in a country. Their within-country difference therefore comes entirely from local prices. Because the largest city is usually more expensive, especially for newly advertised housing, the secondary city will usually receive the higher affordability score. This is a mechanical consequence of the method, not a claim that wages are identical in both cities.

Replacing the national input with city wages would create a different problem. Comparable city-level net medians and prime-age employment rates do not exist across the full sample. Available local figures commonly report gross averages for administrative regions, use different employee populations, and classify commuters inconsistently. A patchwork of those figures would look more precise while being less comparable.

Official 2025 gross-average figures illustrate the possible scale of the caveat:

  • Prague and South Moravia, the region containing Brno: CZK 64,980 versus CZK 51,922 in the fourth quarter, a 20.1% lower secondary-region figure.
  • Bratislava and Kosice regions: EUR 1,960 versus EUR 1,629 in the second quarter, a 16.9% lower secondary-region figure.
  • Warsaw and Krakow, measured in the enterprise sector for the full year: PLN 10,785.02 versus PLN 11,245.73, so the secondary city was 4.3% higher.

Across these three deliberately illustrative comparisons, the secondary-city figure is about 10.9% lower on a simple unweighted average. This is not a KISoL correction factor: the observations are gross means rather than comparable net medians, and two use regions rather than exact city boundaries. They show why the issue matters and why one universal adjustment would be unjustified.

Readers with a credible local wage comparison can adjust the result directly because affordability is linear in wage: adjusted city score = displayed city score × city wage ÷ national wage. For a direct comparison between the two cities, multiply the displayed secondary-to-primary score ratio by the secondary-to-primary wage ratio. As a rough sensitivity check only, an 11% lower secondary-city wage means multiplying its displayed score by 0.89.

This is a genuine caveat, particularly in countries with large capital-city wage premiums such as Slovakia. The national approach remains the most defensible common method because its limitation is visible and consistent; substituting incompatible local wage estimates would introduce hidden error.

This remains a caveat of the city ranking. Readers interested in a comparison that removes the within-country capital-versus-secondary-city effect can check the country/state affordability ranking on the Results page.

Why adjust wages by employment?

A wage statistic describes people who receive wages. It does not capture how likely a working-age person is to be employed. Multiplying by the prime-working-age employment share discounts countries where the published wage applies to a smaller portion of the relevant population.

Low employment can also be economically informative: it may indicate that the prevailing wage is too high for the labor market to clear for part of the population, including cases where a binding wage floor prices some workers out. The adjustment does not identify the cause, but it prevents a high wage among employed workers from standing in for the earning capacity of the whole prime-age population.

Why use new asking housing prices?

The index measures the price currently faced by someone entering the market. Existing tenants or owners may benefit from rent controls, legacy contracts, subsidized arrangements, or purchases made many years ago. Mixing those incumbent prices with current listings would understate the cost of moving into the city today.

Why use road fuel instead of directly pricing more local services?

KISoL uses retail road fuel, not crude oil. A specified fuel grade sold by volume is a closely standardized physical unit across cities. Most services are not. Two quotes carrying the same label can bundle different skill, time, materials, premises, waiting time, regulation, taxes, gratuities, and after-service support. Unless the delivered output is essentially identical, the two prices are not directly commensurable: their difference mixes the local price level with differences in quality and composition.

This is a basic measurement constraint. In Human Action, Ludwig von Mises treats units as interchangeable only where means of the same quantity and quality produce the same quantity and quality of effect. His discussion of index numbers in The Theory of Money and Credit likewise presents aggregation as a useful statistical aid rather than an objective measurement that removes differences between heterogeneous goods. Friedrich Hayek's The Use of Knowledge in Society explains why prices and contract terms embody dispersed knowledge of particular circumstances of time and place, precisely the circumstances that differ between local service providers.

The same problem is recognized in conventional price statistics. The Eurostat-OECD guide to service producer price indices states that prices may be directly compared only when the services are essentially the same and explains that separating genuine price differences from service-quality differences is often difficult. A cheap haircut, repair, lesson, or consultation is therefore not automatically the same service at a lower price.

Road fuel reduces that matching problem while remaining economically relevant. Its retail price combines a common energy input with local taxes, distribution costs, and margins, while energy and transport feed directly and indirectly into service production. KISoL still includes a direct service price where the output can be tightly standardized, but it does not build a broad service basket from labels that conceal different products.

This choice is also tested against observed services rather than assumed. The 28-country validation compares fuel with standardized prepared food, domestic postage, and central-city haircuts. Countries above the sample-median fuel price had a 43.9% higher combined service-price proxy; the combined Pearson correlation was +0.636 and the rank correlation was +0.596. Fuel is therefore used as a quality-consistent directional proxy.

Why exclude healthcare and dentistry?

Sticker prices are not comparable when one country funds care through taxes, another through insurance, and another through direct payment. Waiting time, eligibility, provider access, and quality can also differ substantially. Including only the visible price would reward some systems and penalize others for accounting choices rather than real access.

Exploratory versions that incorporated insurance costs and attempted to account for waiting times produced a large advantage for the United States that was not credibly explained by broader standard-of-living differences. Because the result was highly sensitive to incomparable coverage, access, and quality assumptions, healthcare and dentistry were excluded rather than presented with false precision.

Why not use a crowd-sourced cost-of-living database?

Submission counts, respondent mix, observation dates, and item definitions can vary sharply by city. KISoL instead links inputs to official statistics, retailers, product stores, fuel publishers, real-estate portals, and vehicle marketplaces. Where a direct observation is unavailable, the modelled status is disclosed.

Are all prices direct observations?

No. Some values are direct, some are portal- or retailer-anchored estimates, and some are explicit imputations. The source ledger available on request identifies the status, source organization, URLs, transformation, and limitation for every input. The index should not be read as more precise than those sources allow.

Can manually aggregated listing prices change?

Yes. Inputs that require aggregation, particularly housing, are calculated manually from the current advertisements displayed on the respective real-estate website at the time of collection. They are a dated snapshot of new asking prices, not a permanent average of every property in the city.

The observed result can change as advertisements are added, removed, or repriced. Seasonal differences can also affect the available sample. For example, student cities may show higher new-advertisement rents during the summer or immediately before the academic year, when demand for newly available housing is unusually strong.

The source ledger records the portal and observation period so the calculation can be audited, but reproducing it later may not return the same advertisements or price. These inputs should therefore be interpreted as current market-entry observations rather than timeless city price levels.

What does an index value mean?

The sample median is the reference value. In the affordability view, a higher score means greater income capacity relative to the basket. In the basket-cost view, a lower score means a cheaper basket. Ranks always point in the favorable direction, so rank one is best for the selected view.

Why does the main website exclude microstates?

Very small states can have unusual labor markets, housing supply, commuting patterns, and cross-border consumption. The main view excludes them to reduce these structural outliers. Luxembourg remains included, and a full-sample affordability workbook is available by request for audit.

Is this a relocation recommendation?

No. It is a comparative snapshot built from a deliberately narrow basket. Visa rules, taxes, household structure, crime and personal safety, climate, language, education quality, childcare access and quality, public services, career fit, and personal consumption can dominate an individual decision.

If you are genuinely comfortable with, or indifferent to, every omitted factor and accept the model's assumptions, you may treat the ranking as a narrow relocation signal. That is an unusually demanding condition, and the index is still not a substitute for personal due diligence.
